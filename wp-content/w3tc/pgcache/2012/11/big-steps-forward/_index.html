Big Steps Forward

Written by Tom Allen for the Harpswell Anchor:

For some in Harpswell, October 31 was a date with significance other than for Trick-or-Treating. That date was also the deadline for filing applications with the Maine Charter School Commission for new charter schools.

Harpswell Coastal Academy (HCA) took a major step forward with the submission of its application for a charter school here in town. School design consultant John D’Anieri has worked with an ad hoc group of Harpswell citizens for the past year, helping to guide them, offering advice, and attending informational meetings. But D’Anieri, who is spearheading the application process, says that actually submitting an application has demanded a higher level of specificity and attention to detail than the group has ever had to rise to before.

“The Charter Commission application spells out everything the school will do: curriculum, schedule, food service. There’s a staffing plan, transportation plan, policies on special education, emergency closings. Everything a traditional school would have to do is being defined and spelled out,” D’Anieri says.

President of the Board of Directors of HCA, Joe Grady, says that he has eagerly anticipated this step.

“It feels like a big piece of the puzzle is being put in place. It legitimizes all our efforts. A year ago we were sitting around a table saying, How do we do this? It’s pretty amazing,” Grady says, adding, “It’s one thing to say the school is going to be rigorous, project-based, etcetera. It’s another thing to see it in concrete.”

HCA anticipates hearing from the Charter Commission about their application in January or February 2013. The process will likely include a rigorous interrogation by the Commission during which HCA representatives will be required to defend their assumptions regarding funding, staffing, recruitment and other aspects of their application. If all goes well, Harpswell Coastal Academy will start with grades 6 and 9 in the fall of 2013.

According to D’Anieri, starting small and ramping up is a common strategy for charter schools. It keeps expenses down, allows administration and teachers time to acclimate, and in this case delays the need to find a long-term site to accommodate the eventual 280-plus students and staff.

The issue of finding a home for the school has been a major point of interest for HCA members since the beginning. Town-owned properties such as the former West Harpswell School and Mitchell Field have been mentioned and are being considered as possible sites, but as yet no agreement is in the works. Meanwhile HCA is exploring other possible temporary sites that will fit the school’s limited enrollment for the first year.

“We would like West Harpswell School to be our Plan A,” says D’Anieri, “but we think we have an excellent Plan B for a temporary space for year one.”

Neither Grady nor D’Anieri would reveal the location of this other possible temporary site, saying that negotiations are ongoing and they are respecting the privacy of the current owner. Though they are committed to making the best possible choice for a home, HCA members would clearly like to have the issue settled.

“I for one am tired of having a big question mark there about the site,” says Grady.

D’Anieri says that not having a space nailed down for year one will likely not affect how HCA’s application is received by the Charter Commission, provided the group can show that they have more than one option and that they are aware of the requirements and restrictions a public school building has to meet.

In other developments, HCA is expanding its board of directors. According to Ronnie Weston of Bull Moose Group, who has been engaged to lead the capital campaign and board development, two new members have been confirmed. Victoria Larson, immediate past Chair of Maine Maritime Academy Board of Directors and VP at TD BankNorth has agreed to serve. Joining the board as treasurer is Erin Pelletier of the Gulf of Maine Lobster Foundation (GOMLF). As executive director of GOMLF, Pelletier runs the accounting activities for that organization and also is familiar with grant application and reporting procedures.

“Pelletier’s skills as treasurer will be huge,” says Grady, “not to mention her connections to the lobster community.”

Pelletier is also a former teacher, having taught environmental education.

“I am so excited to be a part of this program. I’m a firm believer that hands-on education makes a big difference in how kids learn—not just sitting in class but engaging with environment,” says Pelletier.

Pelletier first became aware of HCA when the group requested that GOMLF be its fiscal agent. GOMLF has since agreed to serve in that capacity, lending HCA its 501(c)3 status while HCA’s own application, which can take more than 6 months to approve, is pending. Having organized such projects as the Derelict Lobster Gear Retrieval, Salvage & Disposal Project that removes lost lobster traps from the ocean bottom, Pelletier says that she immediately saw how HCA and GOMLF could work together to create projects that will bring kids out of the classroom into Maine’s shores and waters.

“It’s an opportunity I’d never otherwise venture down personally and professionally,” says Pelletier. “I have two kids myself and I would love to have my kids attend a school like this.”

Weston also reports that the group has had a great start to its fundraising campaign with an anonymous friend of HCA donating $50,000, paving the way to pay for a good portion of start up costs, which primarily include professional services needed to create the Charter Commission application. The second phase of fundraising will begin soon, Weston says. HCA anticipates needing another $100,000 or so before opening its doors in the September 2013.
